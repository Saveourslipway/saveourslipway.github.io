Who Owns Clevedon Slipway -

and why does it matter?

Most people don’t care who owns Clevedon Slipway – they just want it made safe and secure for everyone who loves it! We agree. Our primary aim is to work in collaboration with North Somerset District Council and all stakeholders to achieve just that.

The problem is that the money to complete projects like this comes from funding organisations that will only consider applications where the owner can be clearly identified. Quite right too; who would want Lottery Fund or Sports England money going to some shadowy, unidentified private owner?

Somebody must own Clevedon Slipway. Somebody built it, somebody has controlled its use – but who – and why wouldn’t they want to claim ownership?

Well, the trouble is that although Clevedon Slipway is a fantastic community asset, enabling a wide range of leisure activities, in cold, hard financial terms it’s not an asset at all, it’s a liability. It requires maintenance and if somebody is injured on it, they may claim it’s the owner’s fault (though, that’s too simple – that sort of liability to injured third parties has much more to do with “Occupation” than it does with ownership).

To cut to the chase, NSDC say “the title of the land isn’t registered to us, so we don’t own it”. There’s no easy way to say this. We believe that’s just wrong in law. If the title to (ownership of) land is registered to you, then that’s proof you own it – makes it nice and easy when you want to buy or sell your house! It doesn’t work the other way round though. If you aren’t registered with ownership, that doesn’t mean you don’t own it, particularly if title isn’t registered at all.

You don’t need to rely on us on this. If you want to check, look here. This is what the Land Registry itself, a government body, says about it.

Essentially, where nobody is registered with Title it’s a matter of evidence who owns it. It seems a real shame that a public authority should take a very doubtful legal point against the whole community they are supposed to represent, but that’s what’s happened.

“Well,” you might say “why don’t you supply NSDC with the evidence they need to prove they own it?”. Some industrious sailing club members did that three years ago. There’s an incredibly thorough sixty page dossier outlining why, by reference to the indications in the Land Registry blog, NSDC obviously own the slipway. Both the former Leader and an officer were given copies and it’s been expressly pointed out to the current administration. Regrettably there has been no attempt to address it.

One fact that points to ownership is ownership of adjoining land. Why NSDC registered title to the landward end of the slip on transfer from Woodspring but not the seaward end can only be a matter of speculation, but of course the financial risks will have been obvious even then.

We were asked in a comment on a Facebook post, “Why doesn’t the Sailing Club claim ownership?” Well, although as the only slipway user group that has a presence on the seafront, some sailing club members have taken the lead in setting up the Friends of Clevedon Slipway, this group is for all slipway users, organised or casual and we don’t speak for the Sailing Club, but here’s an answer that applies to anyone who might want to claim ownership.

A short history of ownership

  • 1884: Boatman’s Slip built
  • 1895: Clevedon UDC formed
  • 1929: UDC extended the Boat Slip
  • 1971: UDC directed CSC how to extend slipway
  • 1975: Woodspring Leisure Dept discuss slipway reconstruction
  • 1990: Significant repairs to seawall and slipway by Woodspring

But now refuted

  • 1996: 2005: NSC register new ‘red line’, excluding main body of slipway
  • 2016: NSC advise CSC the slipway is ‘nothing to do with them’

First, there’s no way any of the possible groups can prove ownership. They would have to rely on a legal principle known as “adverse possession”. This means that the actual owner knows that the claimant has been using the land without permission and has done nothing about it for an extended period. That just won’t work here. There isn’t an established owner (who admits they are the owner!) who could have done anything about use by others, so there is nobody against whom the possession could be adverse. Perhaps that could be got round, but NSDC have from time to time shut off access so that nobody could demonstrate continuous use. When emergency repair works have been done, NSDC have granted permission, so the essential requirements couldn’t be met. Nor could any organisation prove that they’d had exclusive possession, barring others from access.

Secondly, no small organisation could or should take on the responsibility for an expensive community asset that creates liabilities too big for its balance sheet. No responsible board could risk a group’s assets in that way. No sensible Director or Trustee would risk trading insolvent.

Interestingly, NSDC themselves have already recognised this principle. A look at the Annual Reports of the Marlens Trust on the Charity Commission website reveals that they took a very pragmatic and sensible approach there, retaining ownership of the Marine Lake, granting a lease, indemnifying against risks the Trust couldn’t bear and keeping the liability to the public insured in their name on their policy.

This was a really brilliant move by NSDC to support their community and opened up a hugely successful renaissance for the lake led by the Trust and its resourceful Chair.

That’s fantastic, because it points to a solution! The model can work for all community assets. We at Friends of Clevedon Slipway would be happy to form a Trust to hold the Slipway on the same terms as Marlens hold the Lake.

Better still, we’re all about collaboration and teamwork (anything to do with the risks of the sea needs that) so we’d love to talk about a single Trust to hold all seafront assets (except of course the Pier) on the same terms.

So, over to you, North Somerset, let’s talk!
